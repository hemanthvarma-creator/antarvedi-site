Sri Lakshmi Narasimha Swamy Temple, Antarvedi, is an ancient and revered shrine located at the sacred confluence of the Godavari River and the Bay of Bengal. Established by Sage Vasishta, this holy kshetram is known as Dakshina Kashi — the Kashi of South India.

History & Sacred Traditions

Sri Lakshmi Narasimha Swamy Temple at Antarvedi is one of the most historically significant Vaishnavite temples in Andhra Pradesh, located at the divine confluence where the Vasishta branch of the Godavari meets the Bay of Bengal.

The presiding deity, Lord Lakshmi Narasimha Swamy, represents the fierce yet compassionate form of Lord Vishnu — symbolizing protection of devotees and the destruction of adharma. Devotees believe that darshan at Antarvedi grants courage, spiritual strength, and divine blessings for generations.

Historical records state the temple was rebuilt in the early 19th century by Sri Koppanathi Krishnamma of the Agni Kula Kshatriya community, with major renovations completed around 1823 AD (Shalivahana Saka 1745).

In the Treta Yuga, Lord Rama is believed to have visited this sacred land during his exile to worship Lord Narasimha. In the Dwapara Yuga, Arjuna is said to have meditated here at the confluence to gain divine clarity and warrior strength.

The Antarvedi Theertham festival is celebrated with grand devotion — thousands gather for the Sri Lakshmi Narasimha Swamy Vari Kalyanam on Bheeshma Ekadasi, followed by the magnificent Rathotsavam. Devotees take a holy dip at the Sangamam on Pournami, believing it washes away sins and grants moksha.

Major festivals — Bheeshma Ekadasi, Ratha Saptami, Narasimha Jayanti, and the divine Tirukalyanam — draw pilgrims from across Andhra Pradesh and neighboring states, transforming Antarvedi into a vibrant spiritual hub.
